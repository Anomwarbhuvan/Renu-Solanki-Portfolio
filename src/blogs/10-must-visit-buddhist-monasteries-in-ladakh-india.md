Buddhism is the fourth-largest religion practiced in the world. But, do you know that it found its roots in ancient India and made its way to Asia through the Silk Road (a trade route of ancient Chinese civilization)? Yes, the earliest found evidence of Buddhism is from India, where still a section of people practice it religiously. In this article, we will look at the top 10 Buddhist Monasteries in Ladakh, India.

Home to deserts and two-humped camels, Ladakh is situated in the northern part of India. This high-altitude region rests in the abode of the mighty Himalayas and extends to the Siachen Glacier. The local population is widely dispersed, much like the Buddhist monasteries and gompas located in remote and isolated areas. Ladakh is known for its breathtaking valleys and diverse culture.

The enormous mountains and steep roads have witnessed Buddhism flourish in the region. Buddhism reached Ladakh during the rule of Emperor Ashoka in the 1st or 2nd century AD. However, it became widely accepted in the 7th century when Ladakh became part of the Tibetan Empire.

It was during the 12th century that the construction of monasteries began under the Kadampa subgroup. Some of the earliest monasteries include Likir, Thiksey, and Spituk.

## Top 10 Buddhist Monasteries to Visit in Ladakh

### 1. Hemis Monastery
Hemis Monastery is the richest monastery in Leh and Tibet. It was commissioned by King Sengge Namgyal and built in the early 17th century. Located 53 km from Leh on the southern banks of the Indus River, it is also the largest monastery in the region.

The monastery houses important sections like Lhamokhang, dedicated to the protector deity Paldo Lhamo, and Guru Lakhang, which features an 8-meter-high statue of Guru Padmasambhava. It is decorated with colorful prayer flags, believed to carry prayers through the wind.

The major attraction is the annual Hemis Festival held in June or July, featuring cham dances and the display of giant Thangkas. The monastery also has a museum showcasing ancient artifacts and paintings.

### 2. Spituk Monastery
Spituk Monastery, also known as Spituk Gompa or Pethup Gompa, was built in the 11th century by Od-de. It is located 11 km from Leh and houses around 100 monks.

The monastery includes an assembly hall (Dukhang), temples like Dolma Lakhang and Chokhang, and a reflection hall for prayers. It preserves ancient artifacts, murals, and scriptures depicting the life of Buddha.

The annual Spituk Gustor festival, held in January or February, promotes peace and harmony.

### 3. Thiksey Monastery
Thiksey Monastery is one of the largest monasteries in Central Ladakh, located 14 km from Leh. Built in 1430 by Sherab Zangpo, it houses 10 temples.

The monastery features a 14-meter-high statue of Maitreya Buddha, built to commemorate the visit of the 14th Dalai Lama in 1970. Visitors can attend morning prayers and chanting sessions.

An annual festival is held in October or November featuring cham dances and a local trade fair.

### 4. Diskit Monastery
Diskit Monastery is located in the Nubra Valley and was founded in the 14th century by Changzem Tsera Zangpo. It is the oldest monastery in the region and is situated 114 km from Leh.

The monastery consists of multiple sections including assembly halls, prayer rooms, and residential areas for monks. It houses ancient Buddha images and sacred artifacts.

The Dosmoche festival, held in February, attracts locals and tourists alike.

### 5. Likir Monastery
Likir Monastery, located 60 km from Leh, was established in 1065 by Lama Duwang Chosje. The current structure dates back to the 18th century after the original was destroyed by fire.

The monastery houses statues of Amitabh Buddha, Shakyamuni Buddha, Maitreya, and Avalokiteshvara. It also has a museum displaying Buddhist relics.

Surrounded by scenic landscapes, it is a peaceful destination for spiritual seekers.

### 6. Alchi Monastery
Alchi Monastery is located in Alchi village, about 65 km from Leh. It was founded between 958 and 1055 by Rinchen Zangpo.

The monastery complex includes Dukhang (assembly hall), Sumstek temple, and Majunshri temple. It showcases a blend of Buddhist and Kashmiri artistic styles and houses some of the oldest paintings in Ladakh.

It is also a hub for adventure activities due to its proximity to the Zanskar River.

### 7. Lamayuru Monastery
Lamayuru Monastery, also known as Yuru Monastery or Tharpa Ling, is located 125 km from Leh and is home to around 150 monks. It is famously called the “Moonland of Ladakh” due to its unique terrain.

Founded in the 11th century by Naropa, it features temples, assembly halls, and a meditation cave. The Yuru Kabgyat festival, held between July and August, is known for its mask dances and rituals.

### 8. Phugtal Monastery
Phugtal Monastery is one of the most remote monasteries in Ladakh, built around a natural cave. It was founded in the 12th century by Gangsem Sherap Sampo.

Accessible only by trekking, it offers a unique and adventurous experience. The monastery houses temples, prayer rooms, and living quarters for around 100 monks.

Various festivals are celebrated throughout the year to spread the teachings of Buddhism.

### 9. Stakna Monastery
Stakna Monastery, also known as the Tiger’s Nose Monastery, was founded in the 16th century by Chosje Modzin. It is located 25 km from Leh.

The monastery features an assembly hall with paintings of Buddhist deities and houses around 30 monks. It offers panoramic views of the Indus River and surrounding landscapes.

### 10. Stok Monastery
Stok Monastery, founded in the 14th century, is located 15 km from Leh. It is famous for its library containing 108 volumes of Buddhist teachings known as Kangyur.

The monastery features a large statue of Avalokiteshvara with 1000 arms and 11 heads. It hosts an annual mask festival with dance performances and traditional music.

Nearby attractions include Stok Palace and trekking routes to the Stok Kangri mountain range.

## Conclusion
Buddhist culture has deep roots in Ladakh and continues to thrive through its monasteries and traditions. These monasteries, established centuries ago, still serve as centers for spiritual learning and cultural preservation.

The essence of Buddhism is reflected in the lives of the locals who actively participate in festivals and rituals. Ladakh remains one of the most important Buddhist centers in India, offering both spiritual and travel experiences.

We hope this guide helps you explore these monasteries on your next visit to Ladakh.
