It happened on a lazy Sunday afternoon, the kind where time moves like honey and the world feels half-asleep. She was cleaning out the attic, sorting through boxes of things her mother had saved, report cards with careful handwriting, a pair of roller skates with one wheel missing, and a brass lamp so tarnished it looked like it had been dipped in mud. She rubbed it with the edge of her sleeve, mostly out of habit, and nearly dropped it when a curl of violet smoke rose from the spout. The genie that appeared was not what she expected. He was small, slightly pudgy, wore spectacles, and looked more like an accountant than a magical being. "One wish," he said, adjusting his glasses. "Make it count."

She did not hesitate. She did not ask for wealth or fame or eternal youth. "Make me a kid again," she said. "Just for one day." The genie raised an eyebrow but snapped his fingers without argument. The world blurred and shifted, and suddenly she was eight years old, standing in her grandmother's garden with mud on her knees and the smell of mango pickle drifting from the kitchen. The sky was impossibly blue. The neighbour's dog was barking at a crow. Everything was exactly as she remembered it, vivid and enormous and drenched in the kind of wonder that adults forget how to feel.

> "Childhood is not a time we leave behind. It is a country we carry inside us, and sometimes, all it takes is one small wish to find the way back."

She spent the day doing everything she had missed. She climbed the guava tree in the backyard, scraped her elbow, and did not care. She ate ice cream from the cart that came ringing down the lane at four o'clock, the orange bar that stained her tongue and dripped down her wrist. She played hopscotch until her legs ached, watched fireflies appear in the garden at dusk, and fell asleep on the cool floor of the veranda while her grandmother hummed an old song. When she woke, she was back in the attic, an adult again, the brass lamp cold in her hands. The genie was gone. But something had changed. The heaviness she had been carrying for months, the weight of deadlines and responsibilities and a life that moved too fast, had lifted, just a little. She placed the lamp gently on the shelf, smiled, and went downstairs to make herself a cup of chai.
