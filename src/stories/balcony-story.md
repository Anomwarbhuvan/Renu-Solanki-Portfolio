The balcony was barely six feet wide, just enough room for a plastic chair, a small table, and a pot of tulsi that had somehow survived three monsoons. It faced east, which meant mornings arrived with an almost theatrical display of light, the sun climbing over the rooftops of the neighbouring colony, casting long golden shadows across the clotheslines and water tanks. She had lived in this flat for two years now, and in that time, the balcony had become her observatory, her theatre, her place of quiet witness to the world unfolding below.

Every morning at seven, the newspaper boy on his rusty bicycle would whistle the same tune, slightly off-key but full of conviction. At half past eight, the retired colonel from the ground floor would emerge for his walk, ramrod straight, shoes polished to a mirror shine, as though the neighbourhood street were a military parade ground. Then came the school children in their blue uniforms, a noisy river of laughter and complaints flooding toward the bus stop. She knew their rhythms better than her own. From up here, she was invisible, a silent audience to the small, unrehearsed dramas that made up the life of the street.

> "A balcony is not just a ledge of concrete and iron. It is a front-row seat to the theatre of ordinary life, and ordinary life, if you watch it closely enough, is anything but ordinary."

It was during the lockdown that the balcony stories truly began. When the streets emptied and the world shrank to the size of four walls, the balcony became her only window to the outside. And that was when she started noticing things she had missed before: the elderly couple on the third floor across the road who danced every evening to old film songs playing on a tinny radio; the young boy on the terrace who flew kites alone, his face tilted to the sky as though listening for something only the wind could say. She began writing these observations down, one scene per day, in a notebook she kept beside the tulsi plant. This is the first of those stories.
